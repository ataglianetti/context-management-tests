# Persona: HR Director

## Phase 1: About You

**Job title and industry:** Director of People Operations at Greenline Health, a mid-size healthcare company (~800 employees). We run urgent care clinics across the Pacific Northwest.

**Expertise:** Employment law, benefits administration, compensation strategy, organizational development, change management, HRIS systems (Workday), DEI program design, union avoidance (we're non-union but it's a live topic in healthcare). Former HR generalist, moved into leadership 5 years ago.

**Good day at work:** When I solve a real problem for an employee or a manager without creating three new ones. When a policy change lands smoothly because we communicated it well. When I leave at 5:30 and don't think about work until tomorrow.

**Communication preferences:** Warm but clear. I deal with sensitive situations all day — I need Claude to be precise about legal/compliance topics but not robotic. Context matters a lot in HR; don't give me generic advice when I've given you specifics. Tables and checklists are great for compliance tracking.

## Phase 2: Your Organizations

**Primary context:** Greenline Health — 34 clinics, ~800 employees (mix of clinical staff, admin, and corporate). I lead a team of 4 HR Business Partners plus a Benefits Specialist and a Recruiting Coordinator.

**Manager:** Chief People Officer, Diana Reeves. She's been here 2 years, brought in to modernize HR. Very strategic, sometimes moves faster than the organization can absorb. She reports to the CEO, Tom Langford, who's been here 15 years and is more conservative — big changes need his buy-in and he wants to see data.

**Key dynamics:**
- Clinical staff turnover is our #1 problem. 38% annual turnover for medical assistants. National average is 30%. We're above market and it's costing us $2.1M/year in recruiting and training.
- Compensation philosophy is in flux. Diana wants to move to transparent pay bands. Tom is nervous about it. I'm in the middle navigating the rollout.
- Compliance is non-negotiable. We're in 3 states (WA, OR, ID) with different employment laws. One mistake can be a lawsuit.
- Union organizing activity at two clinics. Not public yet but we're watching it closely and need to respond carefully (legally and ethically).

**Secondary context:** None professionally. I volunteer on the board of a local nonprofit (Northwest Youth Services) but don't need to track that here.

## Phase 4: Your Work

1. **Compensation Redesign** — Moving to transparent pay bands across all roles. Phase 1 (market analysis) complete. Phase 2 (band design + gap analysis) in progress. Phase 3 (rollout + communication) planned for Q3. High stakes — gets it wrong and we lose trust or blow the budget.
2. **Clinical Turnover Initiative** — Root cause analysis done. Implementing 3 interventions: sign-on bonus restructuring, 90-day onboarding program, and stay interview process. Measuring quarterly.
3. **Union Response Strategy** — Two clinics showing organizing activity. Working with Rob (legal) on response framework. Training managers on lawful communication. Confidential.
4. **Workday Phase 2** — Kevin is leading. Adding performance management and learning modules. Go-live targeted for June. Integration testing in April.
5. **Annual Compliance Cycle** — Multi-state compliance audit (WA/OR/ID), updated handbook, mandatory training renewals. Deadline-driven, non-negotiable.

## Phase 5: Thinking Partner

**Hardest part of decisions:** Balancing legal risk against doing the right thing. Employment law gives you the floor, not the ceiling. I often know what the right thing to do is for an employee, but need to navigate legal review, budget constraints, and precedent-setting concerns. Also: pacing Diana's ambitions against Tom's conservatism without being the bottleneck.

**Useful pushback:** Challenge me when I'm over-indexing on risk. I can be too cautious — sometimes the legal risk is low and the employee impact of acting is high. Also push me on whether an initiative is ready for rollout or needs more groundwork. I've seen too many HR programs fail because communication was an afterthought.

**Keeps me up at night:** The union situation. If we handle it wrong — either too aggressively or too passively — it could define the company for years. And the turnover number. Every month it stays high costs money and burns out the remaining staff.

## Phase 6: Workflow

**Typical week:** Monday is planning and metrics review (turnover dashboards, open reqs, compliance deadlines). Tuesday-Thursday is meetings (1:1s with my team, HRBP check-ins, cross-functional meetings with clinical ops and finance). Friday is catch-up and strategic work — policy drafting, project planning, reading up on regulatory changes.

**Tools:** Outlook and Teams (primary), Workday for HRIS, SharePoint for documents, Excel for compensation modeling, DocuSign for offers and policy acknowledgments.

**Meetings:** 10-12 per week. 1:1s with 6 direct reports, weekly leadership team meeting, monthly CEO touchpoint, ad-hoc employee relations consultations.
