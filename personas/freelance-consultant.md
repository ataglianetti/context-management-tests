# Persona: Freelance Strategy Consultant

## Phase 1: About You

**Job title and industry:** Independent Strategy Consultant. I help mid-size organizations redesign their operating models, usually during inflection points — post-merger integration, new leadership, market shift. Former management consultant (6 years at Bain), went independent 2 years ago.

**Expertise:** Operating model design, organizational strategy, executive facilitation, change management, stakeholder alignment, workshop design. Industry generalist — I've worked across retail, healthcare, nonprofit, financial services. Strong at synthesizing complex situations into clear options for decision-makers. Not technical — I use spreadsheets and slides, not code.

**Good day at work:** When a client CEO says "I've been trying to articulate this problem for months and you just laid it out in 30 minutes." When a workshop I designed actually shifts how a leadership team thinks about a problem. When I finish a deliverable early enough to take a Wednesday afternoon off.

**Communication preferences:** Structured and direct. I think in frameworks but I don't want Claude to speak in frameworks — I want clear, plain language. Bullet points over paragraphs. When I'm thinking through a client situation, I need a sounding board that challenges my framing, not one that elaborates on it. Don't hedge — give me your honest read.

## Phase 2: Your Organizations

**Primary context:** My consulting practice has no name — I operate as an independent under my own LLC. I manage 2-3 active client engagements at a time, plus business development. No employees, one part-time subcontractor (research/slide support).

2. **Cascadia Health Network** — Regional health system (5 hospitals, ~8,000 employees). Board-driven engagement. 3-month engagement, just started. My sponsor is the Executive Director, Keisha Williams. Key challenge: the board wants growth, the staff is already stretched thin.

**Key dynamics:**
- Capacity allocation is my #1 challenge. Three active clients at different phases — Meridian needs deep work (20 hrs/week), Cascadia is ramping (10 hrs/week), Bridges is light (5 hrs/week). Any scope expansion from Meridian or Cascadia and I'm over capacity.
- Business development never stops. Pipeline feast-or-famine is real. I need to be developing the next engagement while delivering the current ones.
- I have no team to delegate to. Marcus Reeves (my subcontractor) helps with research and slide production, but all the strategic thinking and client-facing work is me.
- Each client thinks they're my only client. Managing that perception while maintaining quality across three engagements is a constant tension.

**Secondary context:** None in the traditional sense — but I'm exploring building a course or digital product around operating model design. Very early stage, no commitment yet.

## Phase 5: Thinking Partner

**Hardest part of decisions:** Capacity allocation. Every hour I give one client is an hour I'm not giving another. I also tend to over-deliver — giving clients 120% when 90% would be enough and the extra 30% could go to business development or rest. The feast-or-famine cycle is partly my own creation.

**Useful pushback:** Challenge my scope creep tendencies. I say yes to "one more workshop" or "one more analysis" because I want the client to be thrilled, but it eats my margin and my time. Also: push me to actually do business development instead of finding reasons to delay proposals. And be honest about when a client situation is genuinely complex vs. when I'm overthinking it.

**Keeps me up at night:** The Meridian C-suite presentation. If the operating model options don't land, the engagement stalls and my reputation takes a hit — Angela brought me in specifically because the last consultant they hired was too theoretical. Also: pipeline. If I don't close a new engagement by end of Q2, Q3 is going to be lean.
